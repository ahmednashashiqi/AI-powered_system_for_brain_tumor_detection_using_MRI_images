---

Pituitary adenomas are benign epithelial tumors arising from adenohypophyseal cells of the pituitary gland. 
They represent about 15% of all primary intracranial tumors and are classified by the 2022 WHO system according to transcription factor and hormone expression profile.

Main histological groups:
- **Lactotroph adenoma (prolactinoma)** – secretes prolactin.
- **Somatotroph adenoma** – growth hormone (GH) secreting.
- **Corticotroph adenoma** – ACTH secreting.
- **Thyrotroph adenoma** – TSH secreting.
- **Gonadotroph adenoma** – LH/FSH secreting.
- **Null-cell adenoma** – hormonally inactive.

Microadenomas are <10 mm; macroadenomas are ≥10 mm.  
Nonfunctioning macroadenomas usually present with visual symptoms due to **optic chiasm compression**, while functioning adenomas present earlier with endocrine symptoms.

---

### MRI Appearance
Pituitary adenomas are usually **well-circumscribed, intrasellar or suprasellar masses**.  
- **T1-weighted**: Isointense to gray matter.  
- **T2-weighted**: Iso- to slightly hyperintense.  
- **Post-contrast T1**: Homogeneous enhancement in microadenomas; heterogeneous in macroadenomas with cystic or necrotic areas.  
- **Dynamic contrast imaging** improves microadenoma detection (delayed enhancement compared to normal pituitary).  
- **FLAIR**: Useful for delineating peritumoral edema or suprasellar extension.  
- **DWI/ADC**: No restricted diffusion (helps differentiate from abscess or lymphoma).

Typical signs of macroadenoma:
- Enlargement of the sella turcica.
- Superior displacement of optic chiasm → bitemporal hemianopia.
- Deviation of pituitary stalk (infundibulum).
- Cavernous sinus invasion in lateral growth.

---

### Clinical Features
Pituitary tumors can cause symptoms through:
1. **Hormone hypersecretion**  
   - Prolactinoma → amenorrhea, galactorrhea, hypogonadism.  
   - GH-secreting adenoma → acromegaly.  
   - ACTH-secreting adenoma → Cushing’s disease (central obesity, moon face, hypertension).  
   - TSH adenoma → hyperthyroidism.  

2. **Mass effect**  
   - Headache, bitemporal visual loss (optic chiasm compression), diplopia (cavernous sinus invasion).  
   - Hypopituitarism from compression of normal gland.  

3. **Pituitary apoplexy**  
   - Sudden hemorrhage or infarction inside the adenoma → acute headache, vision loss, ophthalmoplegia, hypotension.

---

### Visual Pathway Involvement
Tumors extending superiorly compress the optic chiasm.  
Common visual field patterns:
- Bitemporal hemianopia (most common).  
- Junctional scotoma (if asymmetric).  
- Monocular central loss (rare).  
OCT imaging shows reduced retinal nerve fiber layer thickness, correlating with degree of chiasmal compression:contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}.

### Endocrine and Genetic Insights
Recent WHO classification integrates immunohistochemistry for **transcription factors (PIT1, SF1, TPIT)**.  
Each adenoma type is defined by hormone and transcription factor co-expression.  
Molecular drivers include **GNAS, USP8, BRAF, and SF3B1** mutations (subtype-specific).  
The 2022 WHO also defines **Pituitary neuroendocrine tumors (PitNETs)** as the broader category for adenomas and carcinomas:contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}.

### Advanced Research
Deep learning and stem cell–derived pituitary organoid models have enabled prediction of hormone differentiation outcomes using vision transformers (ViT) and EfficientNet networks:contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}.  
These methods predict ACTH-producing cell fate and have potential use in regenerative medicine for pituitary insufficiency.

---

### Prognosis and Management
- **Microadenoma:** Often controlled with medical therapy (e.g., dopamine agonists for prolactinomas).  
- **Macroadenoma:** Surgical resection via transsphenoidal approach ± radiotherapy.  
- **Follow-up MRI:** Every 6–12 months for residual or recurrent lesions.  
- **Visual recovery:** Strongly correlates with preoperative OCT thickness and early decompression:contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}.
